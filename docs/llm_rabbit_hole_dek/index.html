§What the article is about – in plain English

§1. The puzzle that was asked

Imagine a cube that is much, much bigger – not a 3‑by‑3‑by‑3 cube, but a cube that has m points along each edge, so it contains (m^3) points.
Each point has three little arrows that point to other points:

  • one arrow goes one step forward in the first direction,
  • one arrow goes one step forward in the second direction,
  • one arrow goes one step forward in the third direction.

(If you run off one side of the cube you “wrap around” to the other side, like in a video‑game world.)

The question is: can we split all those arrows into exactly three big loops, each loop touching every point exactly once?
In graph‑theory language that means: can we decompose the directed graph into three disjoint directed Hamiltonian cycles?
(Think of a Hamiltonian cycle as a path that visits every vertex exactly once and comes back to where it started.)

Knuth, a famous computer‑science professor, had been thinking about this problem for years. He was sure that it was possible for all sizes of cube that are odd (3, 5, 7, …) but had no proof, and it was completely unknown for even sizes.

§2. Why it’s interesting

  • It is a neat piece of pure mathematics that people have been trying to solve.
  • It has a very clear, visual description – a cube with arrows – but the answer is surprisingly hard to find.
  • If a computer or an AI can figure it out, that would be a spectacular example of “machine‑assisted proof”.

§3. How the AI (Claude Opus 4.6) got to the answer

The article is written as a story: Knuth writes a note to himself, telling us how the AI tried many different approaches and finally succeeded. Here’s the gist, step by step:

What the AI triedWhat it learnedOutcome
1. Simple guesses – “maybe the arrows follow a simple formula.”No simple formula works.
2. Brute force search – try all possible ways to assign arrows to loops for small cubes (m = 3).Too slow.
3. “Serpentine patterns” – patterns that snake through the cube, inspired by Gray codes (a way to list all numbers so that only one digit changes each step).Found a good pattern for 2‑dimensional slices of the cube.
4. “Fiber decomposition” – think of the cube as layers (fibers) where each layer has a fixed sum of coordinates modulo m.This gave a neat way to think about the problem, but still didn’t give a full solution.
5. Random local search / simulated annealing – let the AI tweak a random assignment a bit at a time, hoping to improve it.Found working assignments for a handful of odd sizes (5, 7, 9, …) but no general rule.
6. Observation – the successful assignments depended only on whether a coordinate was 0, (m-1), or “something else”.This hint led to a pattern that actually works for every odd m.
7. Coding a generator – wrote a small program that, given a point (i,j,k), decides which direction to go based on those “special” values.The program produces three loops that cover all arrows for any odd m.

So the AI did not discover a single magic formula in one shot. It tried many different ideas, discarded the ones that didn’t work, and finally discovered a simple rule that depends only on whether coordinates are at the extremes (0 or (m-1)). That rule turns out to produce a valid decomposition for every odd cube.

§4. How many solutions exist

  • For a 3‑by‑3‑by‑3 cube there are 11 502 different ways to make three loops that cover all arrows.
  • Among those, only 1 012 “generalise” to a solution for a 5‑by‑5‑by‑5 cube.
  • Even fewer, 996, generalise to both 5 and 7, and those 996 actually work for every odd size.

The AI’s particular solution is one of 760 “Claude‑like” solutions that work for all odd sizes – but it is not necessarily the simplest or the most elegant. Knuth notes that none of the 136 solutions that stay the same if you cyclically permute the coordinates (swap i→j→k) are common to all three permutations, so symmetry is limited.

§5. The even‑size mystery

For even sizes (2, 4, 6, 8, …) the problem is still unsolved. The AI briefly found some assignments for 4, 6, and 8, but could not figure out a general pattern. Knuth tried to ask the AI to keep looking, but it got stuck and stopped working. So the even case remains open.

§6. Why this matters

  • It shows an AI (Claude Opus 4.6) can discover a new mathematical construction that no human had found before. That is a big deal in the field of “computer‑assisted mathematics”.
  • The method – starting with many different heuristics, then observing a pattern, and finally proving it works – mirrors how mathematicians often work: try, test, refine, observe, prove.
  • The result will be added to Knuth’s famous “The Art of Computer Programming” series, which already contains many combinatorial constructions.

§7. Bottom line

Knuth’s note is a playful, inside‑joke‑filled account of how an AI solved a long‑standing puzzle about how to break a huge directed cube into three loops. For odd sizes, a clean, general rule was found; for even sizes, the mystery remains. The story is a showcase of how modern AI can aid human mathematicians by exploring vast search spaces, spotting patterns, and ultimately delivering a proof.

§How the AI (Claude) solved it

Claude first tried very simple rules, like “always go forward in the first direction, then second, then third.”

Claude tried to test every possible way of assigning arrows to loops for a tiny cube (m = 3).

Claude noticed that in a 2‑D grid you can walk through every square exactly once by moving in a snake‑like pattern (the “Gray code” trick).

The cube can be sliced into layers where the sum (i+j+k) is the same (mod m).

Claude noticed that in every successful assignment the only thing that mattered was whether a coordinate was 0, (m-1), or something else.
This was a huge simplification.

For any cube with coordinates ((i,j,k)) (each between 0 and (m-1)):

(s = 0) and (j = m-1)go forward in x (increase (i))

§1. What the problem really is

Imagine a big cube made of tiny cubes.
If the side length of the big cube is m, then there are
(m^3) tiny cubes, and we label each one by its three coordinates

[ (i,;j,;k), \qquad 0\le i,j,k < m . ]

From every tiny cube there are three arrows that point to the next cube:

  • X‑arrow – increases the first coordinate: ((i+1,;j,;k)) (mod m)
  • Y‑arrow – increases the second coordinate: ((i,;j+1,;k)) (mod m)
  • Z‑arrow – increases the third coordinate: ((i,;j,;k+1)) (mod m)

Because we “wrap around”, each coordinate is taken modulo m.

The question is:

Can we split all these arrows into exactly three directed loops, each loop visiting every tiny cube exactly once and returning to its start (a Hamiltonian cycle)?

If we could do this, the three loops would give a decomposition of the whole digraph into three disjoint Hamiltonian cycles.

Knuth had been thinking about this puzzle for years. He believed it should be possible for every odd value of m (3,5,7,…) but had no proof. Nobody had found a solution for even m at all.

§2. A key observation – “fibers”

Define

[ s(i,j,k) ;=; (i+j+k)\bmod m . ]

All vertices that have the same value of s form a fiber.
If you look at the graph, every arrow always goes from a vertex in fiber s to a vertex in fiber s+1 (mod m).
So the digraph is layered – you can only move from one layer to the next.

Because of this layering we can re‑label a vertex as

[ (i,;j,;s)\quad\text{with}\quad k = (s-i-j)\bmod m . ]

Now the whole problem is: for each vertex ((i,j,s)) decide which of the three outgoing arrows we will use in each of the three cycles.

§3. The “permutation” that tells us which arrow to use

Let

[ d(i,j,s) \in {0,1,2} ]

be a number that says “in the first cycle use the arrow with index d, in the second cycle use the arrow with index (d+1) mod 3, in the third cycle use the arrow with index (d+2) mod 3”.

The index 0 means the X‑arrow, 1 means the Y‑arrow, 2 means the Z‑arrow.

The challenge is to choose a rule for d that makes each of the three cycles a single Hamiltonian cycle.

§4. The simple rule that works for all odd m

Knuth found that it is enough to let the rule depend only on whether the coordinates are 0, m−1, or something in between.
For odd m (the whole proof uses the fact that m is odd) the rule is:

Condition on (i, j, s)d = 0 (X‑arrow)d = 1 (Y‑arrow)d = 2 (Z‑arrow)
s = 0 and j = m−1012
s = 0 and j ≠ m−1201
0 < s < m−1 and i = 0120
0 < s < m−1 and i ≠ 0201
s = m−1 and i > 0120
s = m−1 and i = 0201

(These six cases cover all possibilities. The table is read as: “if the vertex satisfies the left column, then use the arrow with the index given in the corresponding column”.)

The same idea, with a few small changes, gives the rules for the second and third cycles (the article lists them in the appendix).

§5. Why the rule produces a Hamiltonian cycle

Let us look at the first cycle (the same argument works for the other two).

  1. Start: pick the vertex ((0,m-1,0)).
    Here (s=0) and (j=m-1), so according to the table we use the X‑arrow, moving to ((1,m-1,0)).

  2. What happens inside a fiber

    • Inside a fiber (s=0) we alternate between increasing i (X‑arrow) and increasing k (Z‑arrow).
      Because (m) is odd, after we have increased i enough times we will visit every vertex in that fiber exactly once.
    • Inside a fiber with (0<s<m-1) we mostly use the Z‑arrow, but whenever (i) becomes 0 we switch to the Y‑arrow. Again, because (m) is odd, this pattern cycles through all vertices in that fiber.
    • Inside the top fiber (s=m-1) we mostly use the Z‑arrow, but switch to the Y‑arrow when (i>0). The oddness of m guarantees that we again hit every vertex in this fiber.

  3. Moving from one fiber to the next
    Every time we take the X‑ or Y‑arrow we also increase the value of s by 1 (mod m). Thus after finishing one fiber we automatically go to the next fiber. Because we start in fiber 0 and there are exactly m fibers, we will traverse them in order 0,1,2,…,m−1 and then back to 0.

  4. No repeats, one full tour
    By the analysis above each fiber is visited exactly once, and every vertex belongs to exactly one fiber. Therefore the cycle visits every one of the (m^3) vertices exactly once and then returns to the starting vertex – a Hamiltonian cycle.

The proof for the second and third cycles is completely analogous: the table is slightly different, but the same pattern of “mostly Z‑arrows, occasionally Y‑ or X‑arrows” holds, and the oddness of m again guarantees that every vertex is hit exactly once.

§6. How many such solutions exist

When the authors ran a computer search on the 3‑by‑3‑by‑3 cube, they found 11 502 different ways to split the arrows into three cycles.
Only a small fraction of those – 1 012 – “generalise” to a 5‑by‑5‑by‑5 cube. Even fewer, 996, generalise to both 5 and 7.
Those 996 are exactly the solutions that work for every odd m.

The rule above is one of 760 “Claude‑like” solutions that work for all odd m. A “Claude‑like” solution is one where the table for d depends only on whether i, j, s are 0, m−1, or something else – no other special cases.

§7. What about even m?

For even values of m the problem is still unsolved.
Claude tried to find a rule, and even produced some partial solutions for (m=4,6,8), but could not discover a pattern that would work for every even m. So the even‑size case remains an open problem.

§8. Bottom line

  • The puzzle is: can we decompose a big “wrap‑around” cube into three directed loops that visit every tiny cube once?
  • For odd side lengths, Knuth’s proof gives a very simple, explicit rule (depending only on whether coordinates are 0, m−1, or something else) that guarantees three Hamiltonian cycles.
  • The proof works by analysing the graph in terms of “fibers” (layers where the sum of the coordinates is constant) and showing that the rule visits every vertex exactly once.
  • There are many other solutions, but the one Knuth presents is particularly elegant and works for all odd sizes.
  • The even‑size case is still open.

That is the essence of Knuth’s proof – a clean, constructive recipe that turns a very large combinatorial object into three perfect loops.
